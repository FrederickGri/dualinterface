Feature-preserving manifold approximation 
and projection to analyze single-cell data
 
Yang Yang    1,2,7 , Jialei Gong1,7, Hongjian Sun1,2, Amos Choo3, 
Jessica Cara Mar4, Yunbo Wei    2,5, Yu Zhang2,5, Wenjie Zhang    6, Minglei Shu2, 
Zewen Kelvin Tuong    3  & Di Yu    1,3 
Visualizing single-cell data supports understanding cellular heterogeneity 
and dynamics. Uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) 
and t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE) reveal clustering 
structures but often fail to preserve underlying gene-level information. Here 
we introduce FeatureMAP (feature-preserving manifold approximation 
and projection), a framework that enhances manifold learning through 
pairwise tangent space embedding. By integrating UMAP with principal 
component analysis, FeatureMAP retains clustering structures and feature 
variation in a low-dimensional representation. It presents three key analytic 
concepts: gene contribution, gene variation trajectory, and core versus 
transition states. Gene contribution and gene variation trajectory are 
derived by estimating and projecting feature loadings or variation, whereas 
core and transition states are computationally defined using FeatureMAP’s 
topological properties, including density, curvature and betweenness 
centrality. These concepts enable differential gene variation (DGV) analysis 
that highlights regulatory genes driving transitions between cell states. 
Demonstrated on synthetic and real single-cell RNA sequencing data from 
pancreatic development and T cell exhaustion, FeatureMAP improves 
analyses of trajectories and crucial regulatory genes.
In single-cell data analysis, researchers rely on the intricate task of creat -
ing visual representations capable of capturing the diverse character -
istics of high-dimensional data. As this field has progressed, a plethora 
of dimensionality reduction methodologies have been introduced1–10. 
Notably, uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP)3 and 
t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE)1,2 have become 
particularly prominent due to their exceptional ability to reveal 
clustering structures in single-cell data; potential of heat diffusion 
for affinity-based transition embedding (PHATE)6 further enhances visualization by capturing both local and global nonlinear structure. 
However, the reliability and interpretability of these visualizations 
remain debatable11. These methods are inherently limited in their ability 
to effectively incorporate source features, such as gene-centric details, 
into their visualizations3. Such constraints present crucial challenges 
for researchers in interpreting single-cell data visualization to achieve 
a deep understanding of cellular dynamics and biological mechanisms.

this field to form the gene variation (GVA) embedding, which clusters

highlighting variation-driven trajectories.
Key concepts of FeatureMAP
We introduce three key concepts to illustrate the effectiveness and 
applications of FeatureMAP in single-cell data analysis: (1) gene contri -
bution, (2) gene variation trajectory, and (3) core and transition states.
The first concept, gene contribution, corresponds to feature 
(gene) variation derived from our feature-preserving method (Fig. 1a,c ). 
To formulate ‘gene contribution’ , we explicitly distinguish three related 
but conceptually distinct quantities. ‘Gene expression’ defines a cell’s 
position in the transcriptional state space; ‘expression variability’ cap -
tures isotropic dispersion around that position, describing how broadly 
a gene’s expression varies across neighboring cells without reference 
to direction; and ‘feature variation’ measures anisotropic, directional 
change aligned with local differentiation trajectories, reflecting how 
a gene’s expression evolves as cells transition between states. To put 
it simply, expression corresponds to position, variability to spread, 
and feature variation to directional rate of change along the trajectory 
(akin to ‘velocity’). Consequently, while variance-based approaches 
identify genes that differ between states, ‘gene contribution’ addition -
ally identifies genes whose directional changes precede and drive state 
transitions, revealing regulatory signals that are unavailable from 
expression- or variance-based analyses.
The second concept, gene variation trajectory, is obtained by pro -
jecting the variation vector field to form the GVA embedding (Fig. 1c ). 
This embedding groups cells with similar directional patterns of 
change, revealing fine-scale cellular trajectories. These trajectories 
are consistent with density-based paths identified on the manifold 
via ridge estimation (Extended Data Fig. 2a and ‘Gene variation trajec -
tory’ in Methods). By linking directional gene variation with cell-state 
progression, this concept is particularly effective for identifying genes 
that drive transitions between cellular states, as demonstrated in the 
subsequent case studies.
To identify structured and biologically meaningful cell states, 
FeatureMAP quantifies each cell’s local topology using three metrics: 
density, curvature and betweenness centrality (Extended Data Fig. 1b). 
Density captures local compactness by measuring how closely cells are 
packed, serving as a proxy for state stability. Curvature, computed via 
local gradients of tangent vectors, indicates structural bending: high 
curvature highlights unstable, transitional areas, while low curvature 
marks geometrically smooth, stable regions. Betweenness centrality 
identifies cells acting as intermediaries in the manifold’s graph struc-
ture, quantifying the extent to which a cell bridges trajectories between 
clusters. Together, these measures support a principled classification 
of cells into core states (high density, low curvature, low centrality) and 
transition states (low density, high curvature, high centrality).
The third core concept leverages these topological features to 
define core and transition states in both the expression and variation 
embeddings (Fig. 1c, Extended Data Fig. 1b and ‘Core states and tran-
sition states calculation’ in Methods). In the GVA embedding, these 
states manifest as knot-and-thread patterns, with knots marking stable 
cores and threads tracing dynamic transitions. FeatureMAP labels cells 
as core and transition states by applying quantile thresholds to each 
metric to achieve consensus and refines clusters of core and transition 
states using Leiden clustering16. Compared with UMAP and PHATE, 
FeatureMAP yields clearer separation and improved state resolution, in preserving the topological structure. These methods fall under 
manifold learning, which assumes that high-dimensional observation 
data originate from a markedly lower-dimensional space. Initially, they 
represent this high-dimensional manifold using a nonlinear topo -
logical structure known as a simplicial complex, often implemented 
as a k -nearest-neighbor (k NN) graph. They subsequently preserve 
the distances between neighboring points to compute the embed -
ding. Consequently, the sole topological property retained during 
this nonlinear dimensionality reduction is the distance metric, while 
the feature information becomes implicitly encoded in the distance 
calculations between high-dimensional data points and is lost in the 
embedding space.
Recognizing the need for an improved visualization method in 
single-cell data analysis, we introduce FeatureMAP, which stands for 
feature-preserving manifold approximation and projection, to enable 
the preservation of source features as well as topological structure. 
Specifically, FeatureMAP’s innovation lies in its ability to use the princi -
pal directions of local principal component analysis (PCA) to determine 
the tangent spaces for feature loadings, while maintaining the k NN 
graph12 to approximate the manifold. The resulting visualization of 
FeatureMAP effectively captures clustering structures of the data as 
well as distinct feature-specific insights, providing a more interpretable 
representation of the single-cell data.
These implementations of FeatureMAP allow us to introduce 
three key concepts for single-cell analysis: gene contribution, gene 
variation trajectory, and core and transition states. Gene contribution 
and gene variation trajectory are derived by estimating and projecting 
gene variation, while core and transition states are identified based on 
topological properties, including density, curvature and betweenness 
centrality. These concepts are further extended to differential gene var -
iation (DGV) analysis, which contrasts cell clusters of transition versus 
core states to identify regulatory genes that play important roles during 
cell-state transitions. We demonstrate the utility of FeatureMAP across 
diverse synthetic and real single-cell RNA sequencing data (scRNA-seq) 
datasets, highlighting the importance of preserving feature-level infor -
mation during dimensionality reduction. This enhances the interpret -
ability of single-cell transcriptomic data and supports deeper insights 
into cellular dynamics and regulatory mechanisms.

Overview of FeatureMAP
FeatureMAP is designed to retain both source features and clustering 
structures within a reduced-dimensional space (Fig. 1  and ‘Framework 
overview’ in Methods). While linear methods such as PCA13 jointly cap -
ture data points and feature loadings via biplots14, this interpretability 
can extend to nonlinear manifolds by equipping them with locally 
linear PCA. In this framework, tangent spaces estimated from local 
neighborhoods span the underlying geometry, aligning with princi -
ples from differential geometry15. FeatureMAP constructs a k NN graph 
and estimates local tangent spaces via PCA, where local singular value 
decomposition (SVD) identifies dominant directions to define tangent 
bases and capture both feature loadings and anisotropic radii (Fig. 1a,b ).

to quantify the contribution of each feature within the local geometry:

Building on the three central concepts, we introduce DGV analysis 
to compare consecutive transition and core states and identify genes 
with significant variation (Fig. 1d). DGV quantifies gene-level variation 
between dynamic (transition) and stable (core) states, highlighting 
genes with elevated variability along transition paths—potential regu -
lators of cell differentiation. FeatureMAP jointly embeds cell density 
and gene variation from transcriptomic profiles, producing GEX and 
GVA embeddings for cluster detection and trajectory inference. This 
integrated workflow reveals fine-grained structure, uncovers devel -
opmental trajectories and identifies key regulatory genes, providing 
a robust framework for dynamic single-cell analysis.
FeatureMAP reveals the key regulator of a gene regulatory 
network in synthetic data
Using synthetic scRNA-seq data generated from a bifurcation model 
defined by a known gene regulatory network (GRN; Fig. 2a and ‘Syn -
thetic data generation’ in Methods), we demonstrate that FeatureMAP 
reveals dynamic regulatory patterns with greater resolution than exist-
ing methods. While both UMAP and FeatureMAP successfully recover 
the bifurcation structure (Fig. 2b ), FeatureMAP provides additional 
interpretability: the GEX embedding displays sparser branch inter -
sections due to density preservation, and the GVA embedding deline-
ates regulatory paths via gene variation trajectories. Figure 2 further 
illustrates the rationale behind FeatureMAP’s dual-embedding design. 
The GEX embedding maintains density by projecting cells along ani -
sotropic radii derived from local singular values, which reflect how 
tightly neighboring cells are packed in different directions. This enables 
accurate detection of core states at density peaks (Fig. 2b–d ), but the 
continuity of transitions becomes visually ambiguous, particularly near 
bifurcations. In contrast, the GVA embedding preserves alignment of 
principal directions across tangent spaces, enabling the recovery of 
gene variation trajectories and revealing bifurcating paths with high 
fidelity (Fig. 2b,d). Mathematically, these two properties are orthogonal 
in the embedding space: density preservation depends on scalar radii 
(singular values; Fig. 1b ), while trajectory preservation depends on 
directional alignment (singular vectors; Fig. 1b), and both cannot be 
simultaneously maintained in a two-dimensional projection. This inher -
ent incompatibility motivates the dual-embedding strategy, in which 
GEX and GVA separately encode density and trajectory information. 
Rather than introducing unnecessary complexity, this separation is 
essential for capturing both stable cell states and transitional dynam-
ics within the same dataset.
To assess density preservation, we compared the correlation 
between local radii in original and embedded spaces across methods 
(Fig. 2e and Extended Data Figs. 2b and 4). FeatureMAP GEX achieved 
a high correlation (R2 = 0.660), matching densMAP, and outperformed 
UMAP (R2 = 0.003). Density contour plots identified nine high-density peaks (Fig. 2c ), which aligned with clusters recovered via Leiden clus-
tering16 (Fig. 2d), supporting FeatureMAP’s utility in guiding cluster 
selection. In both GEX and GVA embeddings, we distinguished cells of 
core and transition states (Fig. 2d). Core-state cells corresponded to 
those at density peaks, while transition-state cells bridged them. In the 
GVA embedding, these formed a knot-and-thread topology (Fig. 2d), 
where core states appeared as stable clusters marked by gene signa -
tures exhibiting lower variance than those identified in broader clusters 
(Fig. 2f), and transition states connected adjacent cores.
We next analyze one specific bifurcation branch identified through 
gene variation trajectory (Fig. 2g), which exhibits six core-state clusters 
along pseudotime. Both gene g4 and g6 show highest expression in the 
post-bifurcation cores (clusters 4 to 6; Fig. 2h and Extended Data Fig. 2c), 
marking them as candidate regulators. Conventional differential gene 
expression (DGE) analysis between GEX clusters 1 (pre-bifurcation) and 
2 (post-bifurcation) prioritized g6 due to its greater fold change and 
lower P value (Fig. 2i and Extended Data Fig. 2d). In contrast, our DGV 
analysis, comparing GVA-defined core-state cluster 1 and the adjacent 
transition-state cluster 1, ranked g4 higher than g6 (Fig. 2j). Mapping 
gene variation onto the potential landscape, defined as the negative 
logarithm of local density17, further revealed a peak in both potential 
and g4 variation between clusters 3 and 4 (Extended Data Fig. 2e), pin-
pointing this region as a critical transition zone. Feature projection 
confirms that the influence of g4 precedes the bifurcation, while g6 
becomes active only downstream (Fig. 2k and Extended Data Fig. 2f). 
These findings align with the ground-truth GRN (Fig. 2a), where g4 acts 
as the primary regulator of this trajectory. This example illustrates 
FeatureMAP’s capacity to reveal true regulatory drivers by integrat-
ing variation-based ranking, transition-state topology and potential 
landscape structure.
FeatureMAP effectively reveals regulatory patterns in single-cell 
data by quantifying gene contributions, tracing gene variation tra -
jectories, and distinguishing core and transition states. The follow -
ing studies further validate its advantages in analyzing real-world 
single-cell datasets.

FeatureMAP identifies the trajectory and regulation 
underlying exhaustion CD8+ T cell differentiation in chronic 
infection
FeatureMAP was also applied to analyzing single-cell data for T cells, 
which confront the challenge to define phenotypically and functionally 
heterogeneous populations in various immune responses21. We applied 
FeatureMAP to analyze a specific dataset of mouse CD8+ T cells from 
three time points in a model of chronic viral infection by the lympho -
cytic choriomeningitis virus (LCMV) clone 13 strain22 (Fig. 4), aiming 
to characterize the regulatory mechanisms underlying distinct dif -
ferentiation in T cell exhaustion.
For this dataset, FeatureMAP outperforms UMAP in preserving 
topological structure through density information and gene variation 
trajectories (Fig. 4a,b and Extended Data Fig. 6a). The GEX embedding 
highlights more variable states of proliferating cells (Prolif I and II 
clusters), while the GVA embedding traces the differentiation trajec-
tory from the effector-like population (the Eff-like cluster) through 
the intermediate exhaustion (Exh-Int) population to separated killer cell lectin-like receptors (KLR)-expressing (Exh-KLR) or terminal-like 
(Exh-Term) populations (Fig. 4b ). FeatureMAP identifies core-state cells 
with shared marker genes but lower variability than their correspond -

similar experimental setting of chronic LCMV infection23 (Fig. 4e and

in analyzing regulatory mechanisms underlying dynamic and complex 
cell differentiation.
FeatureMAP enables the definition of the transition-state cell 
population in exhaustion CD8+ T cell differentiation
FeatureMAP enables the identification of core- versus transition-state 
cells in dynamic process of cell development and differentiation, as well 
as revealing potential regulators through DGV analysis. This raises the 
question of whether the transition-state population inferred computa -
tionally can be experimentally detected. The prominent characteristic 
of transition states is high gene variation, but current wet-lab technol -
ogy cannot directly measure gene variation. Therefore, our strategy 
was to screen top-ranked genes in DGV analysis and select those with 
substantial expression in the transition-state cluster. These candidate 
genes may be used to experimentally characterize the transition-state 
population. To be noted, we should not expect cells in a transition 
state ubiquitously and pronouncedly to express a large number of 
marker genes. Otherwise, these transition-state cells would have been 
computed as core states.

FeatureMAP outperforms competing methods in visualizing 
gene dynamics and cell states
To benchmark FeatureMAP’s effectiveness in visualizing gene dynam-
ics and cell states, we compared it against dimensionality reduction 
and trajectory inference methods (‘Benchmark FeatureMAP against 
other methods’ in Methods). Unlike UMAP, t -SNE, densMAP, PHATE, 
local linear embedding (LLE)25 or RNA velocity26,27, FeatureMAP 
uniquely provides interpretable projections of gene contributions, 
capturing both magnitude and direction across differentiation tra -
jectories (Extended Data Fig. 8). In pancreatic data, it clearly resolves 
lineage-defining roles of Arx and Pdx1  (Fig. 3f,g), whereby RNA velocity 
by scVelo27 failed to recover such information (Supplementary Figs. 7–
10). Gene variation vector fields also reveal clearer trajectories, and its 
GVA embedding preserves fine-grained knot-and-thread structures 
(Supplementary Figs. 11 and 12), outperforming UMAP, PHATE and t-SNE. 
FeatureMAP excels in pseudotime inference across synthetic, pancreatic 
and CD8+ T cell data ( R2 > 0.8; Extended Data Fig. 9) and achieves the 
highest silhouette scores and clustering coefficients in defining cells of 
core and transition states (Fig. 6a,b and Extended Data Fig. 10). When 
applied to cell states from Mellon28 and MuTrans29, FeatureMAP shows 
clearer state separation than the original UMAP/ t-SNE visualizations 
(Supplementary Figs. 13 and 14).
Discussion
The central contribution of FeatureMAP is the demonstration that 
source feature information, routinely not preserved by nonlinear dimensionality reduction, can be systematically recovered and embed -
ded alongside topological structure. By grounding feature interpreta -
tion in tangent space geometry, FeatureMAP reframes dimensionality 
reduction from a purely structural task into an interpretive one. In 
scRNA-seq analysis, FeatureMAP treats genes not just as inputs to cal-
culate distance metric, but as explicitly represented elements within 
low-dimensional embedding.
A key implication of preserving feature loadings in low-dimensional 
embeddings is that gene-level hypotheses can be formulated directly 
from the visualization, rather than requiring a separate post-hoc analy -
sis pipeline. In conventional workflows, such as in UMAP, dimension-
ality reduction produces clusters or trajectories that must then be 
interrogated with DGE analysis to identify gene information. Fea -
tureMAP bridges these two steps: gene contributions and variation 
scores are co-embedded with the cellular landscape. Such embedding 
itself already exposes the dynamics of gene changes along with local 
structure in situ, with downstream analyses such as DGV further guid -
ing researchers to identify which genes may drive cell development 
or differentiation. This is particularly valuable for discovering novel 
regulators of poorly characterized biological processes, where exist-
ing knowledge such as marker or driver genes remains very limited.
Besides the advantage of directly visualizing gene dynamics in 
low-dimensional embeddings, FeatureMAP embeddings distinguish 
core and transition states. Unlike existing methods that focus on stable 
and common properties of cell clusters, FeatureMAP defines cells in 
transition states that occupy geometrically unstable positions charac-
terized by high curvature and betweenness centrality. Such transient 
populations are only partially fate-committing to the progeny and 
probably also reversible to the progenitor, which are difficult to capture 
with cluster-based methods30.
Using a FeatureMAP-based pipeline, we identified markers of 
CD8+ T cells in a transition state preceding Exh-KLR differentiation 
in chronic viral infection. This enabled us to physically isolate this 
population by flow cytometry and validate its temporal dynamics. To 
our knowledge, experimental validation of cells in transition states has 
rarely been achieved. The FeatureMAP-based pipeline thus provides 
an opportunity not only to virtually analyze cells in transition states 
to understand the dynamic regulation of genes and pathways in cell 
development or differentiation but also to physically isolate these cells 
for downstream functional applications.
Several limitations of the current study should be noted. First, 
FeatureMAP relies on local PCA to estimate tangent spaces, which 
assumes that the manifold is locally linear within each k -nearest neigh -
borhood. This assumption may break down in regions of high curvature 
or sparse sampling, leading to inaccurate tangent space estimates 
and, consequently, unreliable feature loadings. Second, the intrinsic 
dimensionality d is fixed across all data points, an approximation that 
may not hold for datasets where different regions of the manifold have 
genuinely different intrinsic dimensions, such as quiescent versus
